Summer June 2025

What to do with your lawn in July in Erie, CO

July is when Colorado lawns get stressed and homeowners get confused. Here's how to keep your yard alive without overwatering or burning it out.

Water deep, not often

Short daily cycles train shallow roots. Water 2–3 times per week, longer cycles, early morning. You want the water to reach 6 inches down. Probe the soil after a cycle — if it's dry at 3 inches, you're not watering long enough.

Mow high

Keep your deck at 3.5 inches or higher through July. Taller grass shades the soil, slows moisture loss, and protects roots from heat stress. Don't cut more than a third of the blade at once.

Leave clippings down

Unless you've gone too long between mows, leave the clippings. They break down fast and return nitrogen to the soil — essentially a free, light fertilizer pass.

Fall September 2025

Fall lawn prep for Colorado — what actually matters

There's a lot of conflicting advice about fall lawn care. Here's what actually moves the needle in Colorado's climate.

Aerate before you overseed

Compacted soil — especially on newer builds with clay — blocks seed-to-soil contact. Core aerate first, then overseed into the holes. Late August through mid-September is your window before soil temps drop.

Final mow height

Drop to 2–2.5 inches for your last cut. Too tall and the grass mats under snow and invites mold. Do it gradually over the last two or three mows, not all at once.

Don't fertilize too late

A light slow-release application in early September is fine. Fertilizing in October pushes growth that can't harden off before frost and leaves the lawn vulnerable.

Spring April 2025

Spring green-up guide for Erie and Lafayette

Everyone wants their lawn green the second it warms up. The homeowners who push too hard in spring usually pay for it in July. Here's how to do it right.

Wait to fertilize

Hold off until you've mowed twice. Soil needs to hit 55°F before grass can use the nutrients. Fertilizing into cold soil is just money washed into the storm drain.

Don't mow too early

Let it green up and reach 3.5–4 inches before the first cut. Early mowing on soft, wet turf compacts the soil and tears up the crowns. Wait for a dry day.

Check your sprinkler heads

Walk the yard while each zone runs before the season starts. Winter heaving moves heads out of position. Fix coverage gaps now and your lawn holds up better all summer.

New Builds March 2025

Why your new build lawn looks terrible (and what to fix first)

If you bought in Erie Highlands, Colliers Hill, or any newer Erie or Lafayette subdivision in the last 5 years, your yard is probably fighting against builder-compacted clay soil. Here's what's actually happening and how to fix it.

The soil was destroyed during construction

Heavy equipment compacts the soil 12–18 inches deep. Water can't drain, roots can't penetrate, and sod laid on top of it looks okay for a year then starts failing in patches.

Aerate aggressively for the first few years

Annual core aeration for the first 3–4 years makes a real difference. Pair it with topdressing — a thin layer of compost worked into the holes — and you'll see measurable improvement by year two.

Get a soil test before spending money

CSU Extension offers cheap soil tests. Low pH and nutrient deficiencies are common on new builds. Fix the soil chemistry and everything else gets easier.
