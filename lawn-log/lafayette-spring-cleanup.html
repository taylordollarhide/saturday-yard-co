Spring cleanup: what a full reset looks like

Add hero photo here

This Lafayette yard came through winter rough — matted grass from a late November snow that never fully melted, debris in the beds, and edges that had crept out over the winter. The homeowner hadn't had service since October. Here's what it took to bring it back in one visit.

What we walked into

Colorado winters are hard on turf that wasn't prepped in fall. Matted grass traps moisture and blocks light from reaching the crowns — that's the gray-brown flattened look you see in early April. It's not dead, it just needs to be cleared and given room to breathe.

The full reset process

  • Debris clear first. Sticks, dead leaves packed into bed edges, and anything the snow pushed around. Mowing over debris dulls blades and can fling debris. Clear it before the mower comes out.
  • Rake the matted sections. A light raking breaks up matted crowns and lets air reach the soil. Takes an extra 10 minutes but makes a visible difference within a week as the grass stands back up.
  • First mow at 3.5". Not too low — the turf is waking up and doesn't need stress. Bagged everything on this first pass.
  • First edge of the year. Winter edges creep. The bed lines on this yard had widened by 2–3 inches in places. Re-establishing a clean line took longer than a normal edge pass but sets the baseline for the whole season.
  • Bed cleanup. Dead annuals pulled, debris cleared to the edge of the beds. We don't do planting — but cleaning the frame makes everything look sharper immediately.

One visit turnaround

The before and after here is about as dramatic as it gets in lawn care without a full renovation. A yard that looked abandoned in the morning looked maintained by afternoon. That's what a proper spring cleanup does — it doesn't fix everything, but it resets the baseline so weekly mowing can do its job.

This yard is now on a weekly schedule through October. First mow after the cleanup two weeks later looked completely different — clean, green, and standing up.
